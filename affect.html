Affect — the relational field

Every coded moment carries two affects: the patient's and the clinician's. This page treats affect as relational — not what one person feels, but what happens between two affective fields, and how that predicts change. Dyadic views use the 7,490 moments that render both parties.

1 · The countertransference matrix

When the patient feels X (row), what does the clinician over- or under-feel (column)? Each cell is the clinician emotion's rate given that patient emotion, divided by its overall rate. Warm = over-represented (the pairing is pulled toward this), cool = suppressed. The diagonal is resonance; off-diagonal is complementarity.

2 · Containment vs. escalation

Given a difficult patient affect, which clinician response travels with change? For each patient emotion, clinician responses are ranked by the share of their cases that open (vs. close into impasse). The same patient affect can metabolize or escalate depending on what meets it.

Pairs with at least 8 opening/closing moments shown. Color = % of the pair's cases that open (green) vs close (red).

3 · The affective arc of change — and convergence

Left: mean patient valence across the arc. Opening arcs climb toward the positive; closing arcs stay stuck in the negative. Right: dyadic distance — how far apart the two affective fields sit in valence/arousal space. If opening arcs converge (distance shrinks) toward the end, that is the “moment of meeting” made measurable.

4 · The dyadic circumplex & affective range

Each emotion family placed by valence (x) and arousal (y). A family's color shows who feels it — greener when it is more often the patient's, bluer when more often the clinician's; size = frequency. The clinician's affective field is, on average, more regulated (closer to calm-neutral) than the patient's.
