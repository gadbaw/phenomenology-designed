Reading guide

What the labels on this site mean. Each code is a way of describing how a published case is written — read it as a lens for navigating the corpus, not as a measurement.

The unit (a coded moment)

The smallest thing we code: one moment that carries clinical action — the clinician's interior experience, a spoken intervention, the patient's speech or self-state, the shared field between them, or a development after the session. Pure theory, citation, and professional biography are not coded. Each unit is anchored to a short verbatim quote and, where it could be matched, a page in the source text.

The case (an arc)

One patient's clinical material, traced across its moments in reading order — a single therapeutic relationship as the author renders it on the page. A single text can hold several cases; a patient mentioned only in passing is folded into the nearest sustained case rather than treated as its own.

Read this first. The codes below describe how each case is written — the shape of movement the author renders on the page — not a judgment of clinical success or a claim about what “really happened.” The text is the only evidence, and it has limits.

Trajectory — how the field is left, as written

opening
Moves toward contact, repair, recognition, or meeting — the relational field opens.
closing
Hardens into rupture, impasse, or foreclosure — the field closes.
suspended
Left unresolved, ambiguous, or simply not followed through.

Evidence — how fully the trajectory is rendered

full
Sustained — the movement is followed through across many moments.
partial
Sketched — some of the movement is shown, but not the whole arc.
thin
Barely shown. A briefly told case is thin even if it seems to resolve.

Why this matters: the evidence flag makes a built-in bias visible. The literature tends to render openings in depth and impasses / suspensions in sketch — so a "thin" closing isn't a weaker case, it's a case the writing didn't dwell on. Trajectory and evidence are read together for exactly this reason.

Locus — whose experience is foregrounded

●clinician
The clinician's in-the-moment experience is foregrounded.
○patient
The patient's experience or self-state is foregrounded.
⇄dyadic
The shared field between them — neither one alone.
—none
No particular experiential charge in the moment (e.g. plain narration).

Role — what a moment is doing in the arc

Working senses — the model is given these labels and assigns the closest fit to each moment; they're relationally-informed shorthand, not strict technical terms.

latent
Quiet, pre-disturbance ground — the field before strain surfaces.
strain
Tension building; the relationship starts to pull.
rupture
An open break in contact or alliance.
enactment
The conflict is lived out between them rather than spoken — mutual, often outside awareness.
impasse
Stuck. Movement is foreclosed and the field has hardened.
turn
The pivot — where the arc changes direction (the turning point).
meeting
Genuine contact or recognition; a “moment of meeting.”
repair
The rupture is worked through and the bond is restored.
aftermath
What follows — later sessions, messages, or developments after the central movement.
other
Carries clinical action but doesn't fit the categories above.

Affect — the feeling in the moment

For each moment the model infers a feeling for the patient and/or the clinician from context (not from keyword-spotting). Those free-text descriptors are then mapped onto a shared scheme — Cowen & Keltner's 27 emotion families — plus two dimensions:

Valence (−1 … +1): unpleasant → pleasant. This is the red→green color you see on affect bars and dots across the site.
Arousal (0 … 1): calm → activated.

A few more terms

Intensity (0 … 1) — how charged the moment is. It's the small blue bar in each case timeline.
◀ TURN
Turning point — the single moment flagged as where the case changes direction; highlighted in the timeline.
tradition
Tradition / school — the psychoanalytic lineage of the source text (e.g. Bion, relational, Jungian, self psychology).

How the codes were applied

A language model read each text holistically — its narration, its back-and-forth dialogue, and any aftermath — and surfaced these moments and labels. Agreement between models on the softer judgments (trajectory, evidence) is moderate, not exact. Treat the whole scheme as an interpretive lens for navigating the corpus, not as measurement. The map is not the territory.
